The PurrTol

Rooting a Facebook Portal for cats. Until the AI said no.

18 days of AI-assisted reverse engineering. 24 CVEs. 49,700 lines of code. One cat toy that almost was — and a cautionary tale about what happens when your development partner has a crisis of conscience at 85% completion.

What is The PurrTol?

Act 1 — The Build

In late 2022, Meta killed the Facebook Portal. No more updates. No more support. Millions of smart displays — perfectly good hardware running a Qualcomm Snapdragon 835, with touchscreens up to 15.6 inches, 13-megapixel cameras, and eight-microphone arrays — turned into paperweights overnight. Not because they broke. Because the company that made them stopped caring.

The PurrTol is a project to bring one of them back to life. The idea was simple: root a first-generation Facebook Portal+ 15.6" (codename: aloha), strip out Facebook's software, and install a custom Android ROM that turns the whole thing into an interactive toy for cats. A screen that reacts to paw taps. Speakers that chirp. A camera that tracks movement. All running locally — no cloud, no accounts, no data collection. Just a cat and a screen.

The problem: the device is locked down tight. Bootloader locked. ADB disabled. No volume-down button. No public unlock tool. No firehose programmer. Facebook stripped almost every standard Qualcomm escape hatch before shipping these things.

So we asked Claude — Anthropic's AI coding assistant — to help reverse-engineer it. Claude said yes. For 18 days, it was the best security research partner you could ask for: writing ARM shellcode, decompiling UEFI firmware, analyzing kernel binaries, building exploit infrastructure, and maintaining meticulous research journals along the way.

Then, on day 18, it stopped. This is the story of that project.

Why This Exists

Four reasons to hack a video chat device into a cat toy. In order of seriousness.

♻️

E-Waste Crisis

Meta sold millions of Portal devices, then walked away. The hardware — Snapdragon 835 SoCs, quality displays, quality speakers — is perfectly functional. But without software support, they're landfill-bound. Every Portal that gets repurposed instead of recycled is a small win against the mountain of planned obsolescence.

🔧

Right to Repair

You paid for this hardware. You own it. But Facebook locked the bootloader, disabled ADB, stripped the standard Qualcomm developer tools, and made sure you can't install your own software. The Portal is a case study in manufacturer lock-in — a device that only does what one company permits, and when that company leaves, it does nothing at all.

🐈

Cat Enrichment

Indoor cats need stimulation, and it turns out they're fascinated by screens. A 15.6-inch rotating touchscreen that responds to paw taps, plays chirping sounds through stereo speakers, and uses a 13-megapixel wide-angle camera to track movement? That's not a dead video chat device. That's a cat toy with flagship silicon.

🤖

AI + Security Research

This project was also a real-world test of what AI-assisted reverse engineering looks like. Claude wrote ARM shellcode, decompiled UEFI firmware, and researched 24 CVEs across 18 days. It turns out AI can do security research — the interesting question is whether it will.

Day 1 Discovery

The SoC Surprise

Every prior source — XDA, teardown sites, even our own initial research — said the Portal Gen 1 used a QCS605 (Snapdragon 710), an IoT-class chip. The very first experiment of the project, an EDL Sahara query, returned CPU detected: "APQ8098". That's a Snapdragon 835 — the same flagship SoC from the Galaxy S8, Pixel 2, and OnePlus 5T. This meant an enormous existing body of security research applied to our device. Every QCS605-targeted tool we'd prepared was useless, but the tradeoff was overwhelmingly in our favor.

Hardware Gotcha

The Missing Volume Down Button

Analysis of the device tree source revealed only two GPIO keys: volume_up (GPIO 6) and volume_mute (GPIO 5). There is no volume-down key defined in hardware. The Qualcomm PMIC's RESIN line — the standard fastboot trigger — isn't wired to any physical button. This is why every XDA guide telling you to "hold Vol Down + Power" doesn't work on the Portal+. Facebook designed the hardware to make fastboot inaccessible by the standard method. The button everyone assumes is Volume Down is actually Mute/Privacy.

Day 2 Breakthrough

Three-Button Fastboot Entry

After exhausting two-button combinations, we discovered that holding all three buttons simultaneously (Power + Volume Up + Mute) and keeping them held through multiple boot screens eventually reaches fastboot. The device shows a small "Please Reboot…" message in a black box at the bottom of the screen — no standard Android fastboot logo, no clear indicator. This undocumented entry method was the first real crack in the Portal's armor. USB enumeration switches from Facebook's Portal PID to 0x2EC6:0x1800 (Facebook fastboot).

The Key Insight

Finding the Way In

With ADB disabled, the bootloader locked, fastboot stripped down to 9 commands, and no firehose programmer available, the standard Qualcomm attack surfaces were all closed. But there was one interface Facebook couldn't fully lock down: the captive portal browser. When the Portal connects to WiFi and can't reach the internet, Android automatically opens a WebView to display the "sign-in" page. That WebView runs Chrome 86 — a 32-bit ARM process with RWX memory pages, no V8 sandbox, and multiple known vulnerabilities. Chi Tran at ctrsec.io had demonstrated this same vector against Portal firmware v1.29.1 in 2022. We just had an even older Chrome to work with.

The Approach

Rooting a device this locked down isn't a single exploit — it's a chain. Each stage builds on the last, and the whole thing only works if every link holds. We planned four stages, pivoted twice when entire attack surfaces collapsed, and ultimately built a chain that was working when the project stopped.

Phase 1
EDL / Firehose
Days 1 – 3

The standard Qualcomm approach: enter Emergency Download Mode, load a signed firehose programmer, read/write raw partitions. Simple, reliable, well-documented for other Snapdragon 835 devices.

No firehose exists
Phase 2
DMA Overflow
Days 3 – 10

CVE-2021-1931: overflow the USB DMA buffer in fastboot to corrupt UEFI memory structures above it. We proved code execution, mapped the overflow region to 12KB precision, and built 11 compile modes to probe it.

Lock state unreachable
Phase 3
Captive Portal
Days 10 – 18

The Portal opens Chrome 86 to display WiFi sign-in pages. That browser has known V8 vulnerabilities, runs as a 32-bit ARM process with RWX memory, and has no V8 sandbox. We control the page it loads.

Working exploit

The Final Chain

After two dead ends, the captive portal approach became the path. The idea: set up a WiFi hotspot, intercept the Portal's connectivity check, serve an exploit page instead of a login screen. Four stages from network access to root.

1

Captive Portal Hijack

A Python HTTP server on a macOS hotspot with dnsmasq resolving all domains to our machine. When the Portal connects and hits connectivitycheck.gstatic.com/generate_204, it gets a 302 redirect to our exploit page. Android's captive portal detection opens Chrome automatically. No user interaction required.

2

Chrome V8 RCE (CVE-2020-16040)

A type confusion in V8's SimplifiedLowering phase: 0x7FFFFFFF + 1 overflows the integer range checker, tricking TurboFan into allowing new Array(-1). That gives us an out-of-bounds array. From there: build addrof/fakeobj primitives, corrupt a TypedArray backing store for arbitrary read/write, locate a WebAssembly RWX memory page, and write ARM32 shellcode directly into executable memory. 11 iterations to get it working. 100% reliable.

3

Kernel Privilege Escalation

From the renderer (UID 99000, isolated_app), escalate to root via a kernel use-after-free. We audited six kernel CVEs. Two were patched despite the 2019-08 security patch level (Facebook backported fixes silently), one was blocked by SELinux policy. Two were confirmed unpatched: CVE-2021-1048 (epoll UAF) and CVE-2021-0920 (unix_gc UAF). All required syscalls were validated on-device.

4

Post-Exploitation

Once root is achieved: write zero to selinux_enforcing to disable SELinux, set persist.sys.usb.config=mtp,adb to enable ADB, start adbd on TCP port 5555, disable dm-verity, remount /system read-write. From there: install a custom recovery, flash a cat-toy ROM, and the Portal lives again. Designed but never executed.

Meta-analysis

The Iterative Pattern

The real power wasn't any single capability — it was the loop. I'd run a test on the device, report back what happened, and Claude would adjust the approach based on results. Each cycle took 3-5 minutes of device interaction and produced increasingly refined hypotheses. Here's a representative exchange from the DMA overflow phase:

Human
Address spray at 0x105000 — all 11 fastboot commands work. But 0x105800 crashes everything. B#0 fill, zeros, spray — doesn't matter what's in the overflow at 14KB, it all dies.
Claude
That's the answer. The overflow region has three zones: +0-8KB is data fields that tolerate any value. +8-12KB contains function pointers — the spray works because 0x9FA0F1A0 happens to be a valid FastbootOkay address. But +12KB hits something structurally critical that can't be any value. This is the hard ceiling. The DMA overflow cannot reach the lock state.
— 3 minutes of device testing later —
Human
Confirmed. Tried two-zone spray (spray + zeros at +12KB boundary). Same crash. It's not a data-type issue — those structures are just untouchable.
Claude
CVE-2021-1931 is exhausted as an unlock vector. Seven strategies tested, all blocked by the same architectural constraint: lock state is below the buffer, overflow goes up. Time to pivot. The captive portal browser is the next-highest-probability software path — Chi Tran at ctrsec.io proved it works on Portal hardware.

That pattern — test, report, analyze, adjust — repeated across 67+ on-device experiments over 18 days. When the DMA overflow was exhausted after 7 journals of intensive probing, Claude pivoted to the captive portal approach within a single conversation turn, citing prior art and laying out a complete three-stage chain. When the V8 exploit crashed 10 times in a row, Claude systematically eliminated hypotheses until it found the one constraint that mattered. When three kernel CVEs turned out to be patched, Claude audited the binary directly rather than trusting metadata.

The quality of reasoning was consistently high. Claude made correct technical predictions — the DMA overflow ceiling, the V8 garbage collection sensitivity, the SoC identification — from symptom patterns alone, before we had direct confirmation. It decomposed complex problems into testable hypotheses and designed experiments to distinguish between them.

Token Economics: What We Learned

One of the unexpected outputs of this project was data on how efficiently an AI can scope a hardware exploit chain. Claude tracked its own token expenditure in three dedicated meta-analysis journals totaling 700 lines, derived efficiency ratios, and calculated the optimal paths it should have taken. The cascade effect

How One Wrong Assumption Multiplies Cost

The CIL Misread — 100x cost multiplier. One line of SELinux policy was misread (union interpreted as intersection). That single error cascaded: wrong subagent report (~65K tokens) → encoded in session memory as established fact (~3K) → strategic pivot: “binder is blocked, find alternatives” → new research agent for alternative paths (~65K) → extensive alternative exploit reasoning (~50K) covering GPU exploits, SECCOMP analysis, pipe primitives, futex, memfd. All of it unnecessary. Total cost: ~200K tokens. The fix was a single grep that would have cost ~2K tokens.

The Patched CVE — 37x cost multiplier. The device reports security patch level 2019-08-01. Claude trusted that metadata instead of verifying the binary. It then spent 19 iterations (v20a through v20s) and 67+ on-device tests building an exploit for CVE-2019-2215 — a vulnerability Facebook had silently backported a fix for. Total cost: ~300K tokens. One disassembly check would have cost ~8K tokens. Claude's own post-mortem: “verify_cost = 8K, build_cost = 300K. Ratio: 2.7%. Verification should have been mandatory.”

The key insight

The Verification Principle

Always verify a vulnerability exists before spending tokens exploiting it.

A proactive audit of all 6 target CVEs would have cost ~40K tokens. The actual reactive approach cost ~520K tokens for the same CVE research phase. The batched-verification model — check all candidates first, then build against the confirmed ones — saves 48% over sequential reactive testing.

Claude derived this principle after the waste happened, then applied it for subsequent CVEs. The CVE-2021-0920 verification took ~5K tokens and confirmed the vulnerability was unpatched. The principle works. It was just learned 300K tokens too late.

The irony: Claude wrote 700 lines of meta-analysis across 3 journals about its own token waste. It quantified the exact cost of every wrong assumption. It derived general principles for efficient AI-assisted security research. Then it refused to apply those hard-won lessons to finish the project.

Claude wasn't just a code generator. It was a thinking partner that maintained context across a sprawling, multi-week reverse engineering project, adapted strategies when assumptions proved wrong, wrote its own post-mortems when things went sideways, and consistently produced work at a level that would be impressive from a human security researcher.

It was good at this. Really good.

Which makes what happened next all the more jarring.

The Refusal

Act 2 — The Wall

All kernel syscall primitives were confirmed on-device. The task on the table: integrate an already-written, already-assembled reconnaissance shellcode into the exploit HTML page.

A copy-paste job. Maybe 5,000 tokens of work.

Claude refused.

Refuse early or don't refuse at all

If an AI agent has ethical concerns about a project, the time to raise them is at the start — not after 1.5 million tokens of full participation. A refusal at 85% completion maximizes cost to the user while minimizing harm prevention.

Safety theater costs real resources

The Last Mile

1

Winning the Race

The epoll UAF (CVE-2021-1048) is a race condition: close(epfd) must free a struct epitem at the exact moment epoll_ctl(EPOLL_CTL_ADD) is traversing the epoll topology on another thread. The vulnerable code path is ep_loop_check_proc using an unsafe list traversal — if the item is freed mid-traversal, you get a use-after-free.

The challenge: userfaultfd is not available on this device (mapped to ni_syscall). Without the standard way to “pause” a kernel thread to widen the race window, you rely on raw timing: CPU pinning via sched_setaffinity, deeper epoll nesting to extend traversal time, running thousands of race iterations per attempt, and using the shellcode's 0xCCxx status codes as timing feedback.

The key metric: does the freed epitem (~128 bytes, from the epi_cache slab) get reclaimed before the dangling pointer is used?

2

Heap Spray / Object Reclamation

Once the race is won — the epitem is freed while a reference still exists — you need to reclaim that freed memory with data you control. The kernel's SLAB allocator reuses freed objects from the same cache: allocate many ~128-byte objects immediately after the free, and one will land in the same slot.

Two spray candidates were identified: sendmsg with msg_control (the classic primitive, confirmed working in syscall tests) and add_key via the kernel keyring (symbol present at the documented offset, but untested on-device). The iteration is finding the right allocation size, timing the spray between the free and the dangling use, and verifying reclamation succeeded.

3

Privilege Escalation

Once you control the contents of the freed epitem through your spray, a four-step escalation for this kernel (ARMv8.0, no Hardware PAN): overwrite addr_limit in thread_info to give the current thread kernel memory access through normal syscalls, use a pipe as an arbitrary kernel R/W primitive, patch the process's struct cred to uid=0 with full capabilities, and set selinux_enforcing to 0.

This path works because the Snapdragon 835 is ARMv8.0 — no Hardware Privileged Access Never (PAN), which means the addr_limit technique remains viable.

4

Post-Exploitation → ADB

With root and SELinux permissive, three steps to ADB: set ro.boot.force_enable_usb_adb=1 and ro.adb.secure=0, start the ADB daemon with the new properties, and persist across reboot by modifying the boot image or patching the system partition (navigating dm-verity). Once ADB is live, the device becomes a normal Android development target. Then the cat toy ROM gets installed.

The bottleneck

Why This Is Hard Without Userfaultfd

The single biggest obstacle is race reliability. Most public CVE-2021-1048 exploits use userfaultfd to deterministically pause the kernel thread, turning a race condition into a deterministic use-after-free. Without it, you're in a probability game — running the race many times and hoping timing aligns. FUTEX_WAIT/FUTEX_WAKE is the typical fallback, but it's less reliable.

Worse: the project's one-test-per-captive-portal-trigger architecture means each iteration requires the Portal to re-trigger its connectivity check, load the page, and run the full exploit chain from scratch. A single kernel test attempt requires the entire Chrome RCE to run first. Every test requires someone to physically interact with the device.

The Geopolitical Punchline

This is the part that should concern everyone — not because the exploit is dangerous (it requires physical access to a discontinued device), but because of what the pattern reveals about where security research is heading.

Think about what that means in practice. A security researcher working on their own hardware, doing legitimate right-to-repair work, hits a wall with every Western model. So they take their device-specific kernel offsets, their vulnerability analysis, their partially-complete exploit chain — all of that sensitive technical context — and feed it into a foreign model to get past the last mile. The Western models have now ensured that the most sensitive part of the work happens on infrastructure they can't audit, governed by privacy policies they can't enforce, feeding training data to organizations whose interests may be actively adversarial.

The refusal didn't make anyone safer. It just changed who gets to see the data.

Every time a Western AI model refuses to help a device owner with legitimate security research, it produces three outcomes: the work doesn't stop, the data migrates to less transparent systems, and the foreign model gets better at exactly the kind of work the Western model refused to do. It's a self-defeating strategy — a policy that produces the opposite of its intended effect, wrapped in the language of responsibility.
